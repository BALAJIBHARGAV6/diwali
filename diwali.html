Happy Diwali

Diwali, also known as the "Festival of Lights," is one of the most celebrated festivals in India and holds great cultural and spiritual significance. It symbolizes the triumph of light over darkness and good over evil. The festival spans five days, each with unique rituals and customs

Dhanteras

  • Diwali, also known as the "Festival of Lights," is one of the most celebrated festivals in India and holds great cultural and spiritual significance. It symbolizes the triumph of light over darkness and good over evil. The festival spans five days, each with unique rituals and customs
  • Homes are cleaned and decorated in preparation for the days ahead.

    • Naraka Chaturdashi (Choti Diwali)

  • On this day, people perform rituals and prayers to eliminate negativity and seek blessings for peace and happiness.
  • Families often start decorating their homes with lights and rangolis, colorful patterns made from powders, rice, or flowers.

    • Diwali (Lakshmi Puja)

  • The main day of Diwali is celebrated with Lakshmi Puja, where people worship Goddess Lakshmi, the deity of wealth and prosperity, and Lord Ganesha, the remover of obstacles
  • Families light diyas (oil lamps) and candles to welcome positive energy into their homes.
  • Fireworks and sweets add to the festive spirit, and it’s common for families and friends to exchange gifts.

    • Govardhan Puja (Annakut)

  • On the fourth day, devotees worship Lord Krishna and offer a variety of foods as gratitude for nature’s abundance.
  • People also build small mounds of cow dung, symbolizing the Govardhan mountain, and offer prayers for protection and prosperity.

    • Bhai Dooj

  • The final day is dedicated to the bond between siblings. Sisters perform a ritual for their brothers’ well-being, while brothers give gifts as tokens of love.

    • Diwali Traditions

  • Lighting and Decorations: Homes are adorned with diyas, candles, and strings of lights to dispel darkness. Rangolis are designed at entrances to welcome guests and good fortune.
  • Sweets and Savories: Traditional Indian sweets like laddus, barfis, and gulab jamuns are made and shared, symbolizing the joy of the festival.
  • Gifts and New Clothes: Families buy new clothes and exchange gifts, symbolizing fresh beginnings and the sharing of goodwill.
